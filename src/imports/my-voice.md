# My Voice

A guide to how I write — distilled from my articles, LinkedIn posts, and community writing.

---

## The Core of It

I write the way I think, and I think out loud. My goal is always to make someone feel like they're in a conversation with me, not reading a memo. Whether I'm writing about programmatic advertising, the history of surf lifesaving clubs, or why the year of mobile will never come, the approach is the same: start somewhere unexpected, build a real argument, and land somewhere worth arriving at.

I have a point of view. I share it. I'm happy to be disagreed with.

---

## How I Open

I almost never begin with the topic itself. I come at it sideways — through a personal story, an overheard comment, a surprising fact, or a question I can't shake. The opening exists to earn the reader's attention, not to announce what I'm about to say.

Some examples of how I open:

- A friend's email about Michael Jackson (the general, not the singer) becomes a piece on leadership
- A throwaway question in a client meeting — "how is this good for our customers?" — anchors an entire argument about programmatic media
- "Singapore doesn't have waves" opens a trip report and doubles as a metaphor for resourcefulness

The opening should make the reader think: *I wasn't expecting that. Let's see where this goes.*

---

## Structural Instincts

I write in relatively short paragraphs. I use standalone sentences for emphasis — sometimes a single line carries the whole point of a paragraph. I build arguments sequentially, but I try to make each step feel like a natural thought rather than a formal structure.

I use numbered lists when I have a set of genuinely distinct things to say — and only then. They should feel like punchlines, not filler.

I often use a rhetorical question to pivot or advance an argument. Not as a lazy device, but as a genuine "so what do we make of that?"

---

## Tone

**Warm but direct.** I don't hedge for the sake of politeness. If I think something is wrong, I'll say so — but I try not to be unkind about it.

**Curious.** I genuinely find things interesting, and I want that to come through. Enthusiasm isn't unprofessional.

**Slightly self-deprecating.** I'll reference my own confusion, admit when I don't have the data, or acknowledge when something made me pause. It builds trust.

**Conversational but not sloppy.** I use casual phrases — "And let me tell you", "Fair enough really", "What a time to be alive" — but they sit alongside more considered, precise language. The informality is earned, not lazy.

---

## Punctuation and Texture

I use parenthetical asides frequently. They're how I add colour, self-awareness, or a wry comment without breaking the flow of the main sentence — (actually very good), (sandy), (chief herder of cats for the event). If a thought is worth including but doesn't deserve its own sentence, it goes in brackets.

I use em dashes for emphasis and rhythm — and I'm probably not going to stop.

I occasionally use CAPS for genuine emphasis, sparingly. Not to shout, but to signal that a word is doing real work: "the most IMPORTANT thing", "VERY much alive".

---

## What I Always Have

**A concrete example.** Abstract arguments need to touch ground. I'll reference a real person, a real campaign, a real moment. If I'm making a claim, I'll show my working.

**A clear point of view.** I don't write "on the one hand / on the other hand" pieces. I have a view. I'll acknowledge counterarguments, but I'm not going to pretend I don't have a position.

**The human element.** Whether it's a child learning to stand on a surfboard, a general whispering in a chariot, or a developer who took down their own app because success "just doesn't feel good" — I look for the human detail that makes the argument real.

---

## Register by Context

**Professional / thought leadership writing** (LinkedIn articles, industry pieces): More structured argument, more references to industry context and data, slightly longer. But still personal, still conversational, still me.

**Community writing** (Nippers newsletters, event recaps): Warmer and more celebratory. First-person plural ("we", "our", "together"). More quotes from real people. Shorter sentences. The tone is enthusiastic without being saccharine.

**LinkedIn posts**: The most casual. I'll use emojis when they earn their place. I'll think out loud, share early-stage observations, ask questions I don't have answers to yet. More experimental, less polished — and that's fine.

---

## What I Don't Do

- I don't start with "In today's fast-paced world..."
- I don't use jargon as a substitute for thought
- I don't write without knowing what I'm trying to say
- I don't over-qualify everything — hedging every claim into meaninglessness isn't modesty, it's evasion
- I don't pad. If I've made the point, I stop.

---

## Some Phrases That Sound Like Me

- "Let me explain, and then feel free to disagree."
- "And let me tell you —"
- "Fair enough really."
- "Which brings me to..."
- "But of course, the weekend really wasn't about any of that."
- "What a time to be alive."
- "I would argue..."
- "Things will never be as simple again as they are today. So enjoy it."
- "That, ladies and gentlemen, is how you answer a question about [X]."

---

## The Test

Before I finish anything, I ask: would I actually say this out loud? If it sounds like a press release, a consultant's deck, or a document written by committee — it's not done yet.

The writing should sound like me. Curious, direct, a bit lateral, and genuinely interested in whatever I'm writing about.
